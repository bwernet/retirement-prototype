# Challenge

## Retirement isn't one decision — and no tool connects the ones it is

For Americans 50+, retirement is a web of interconnected choices across
finances, healthcare, housing, and lifestyle. The landscape they navigate it
with is deeply fragmented:

- **Scattered** advice across Google, PDFs, and advisors
- **Hidden** details behind paywalls and sales funnels
- **Siloed** services that never show how one decision impacts another

Users were confused, overwhelmed, and hesitant to act — so they either delayed
key decisions or paid high-cost advisors. For Silvur, that gap was the
opportunity: become the trusted, go-to source for planning and unlock a
massive, underserved market.

[HYPOTHESIS CALLOUT BOX — visually distinct, boxed]

**Our hypothesis** — If we created a transparent, interactive experience that
helped users understand how retirement decisions interconnect, we could build
confidence, drive engagement, and win a segment that feels overlooked.

# Approach

## Discovery: users kept re-opening decisions the industry called "done"

I led product discovery from the ground up — planning and running user
interviews, synthesizing insights, and working with our PM to define
hypotheses we could test with real users before we built. With engineering, I
prototyped early to test feasibility and usability.

Initial MVPs explored financial milestones and lifestyle goals. Users kept
asking a harder question:

[PULL QUOTE — largest text on the page]
**"How does this all fit together?"**

Their decisions weren't a sequence. They were a web — financial, emotional,
and logistical at once — and people revisited them every time life changed.

[EMBED: decision-web (research artifact)]
[Its built-in caption closes the section: "Every tool answered a box. No tool
answered the lines between them — that became Silvur's product thesis."]

[IMAGE ROW: three interview stills, captioned:]
The value prop in a participant's hand motions: information on retirement is
everywhere → put it all in one place → hand me a list of what to do.

Users didn't want to *see* the complexity — they wanted the product to hold it
for them and hand back simple next steps. The engine had to be connected so
the guidance could be simple.

## Finding the hook: the Retirement Score

That insight became our first breakthrough. The **Retirement Score** takes a
few simple questions about a user's age and finances and turns them into a
bird's-eye view: how long your money could last, and how each interconnected
decision — retirement age, Social Security timing, spending — moves the
outcome.

[EMBED: retirement-score prototype]
[Caption:] A working prototype of the Retirement Score — every input around
the phone recalculates the score, the income chart, and how long money lasts.

- **Tradeoff:** We debated going deeper into simulations, but prioritized
  simplicity and clarity to reduce cognitive load.
- **Result:** The top-performing feature in user surveys and the primary
  driver of downloads and early adoption.

And the re-opening behavior we saw in research became a feature, not a bug —
the Score is built to come back to:

[USER QUOTE — styled as testimonial]
"[The Retirement Score] helps me see where I stand — like, okay, now I get
where I'm at. Then I can dig in, learn more, make some decisions, and even
come back and tweak things based on what I've learned."

## Retirement School: retention through education, not budgeting

With adoption proven, we focused on retention.

- **What we tested:** I led a cross-functional ideation sprint, then prototyped
  two frontrunners — daily financial tracking (budgeting, saving) and
  bite-sized educational content.
- **What we learned:** budgeting needs were already met elsewhere; education
  was the gap.
- **What we built:** **Retirement School** — a library of short, mobile-first
  lessons focused on the highest-stakes decision points: Medicare, Social
  Security, 401(k) withdrawals. I led content design, UX, and interaction
  patterns, launching iteratively against engagement data.
- **Result:** the largest driver of the 7.5x growth in time spent in app.
